## Before Damage Happens: Prepare Now

The best time to understand your insurance coverage is before you need to file a claim. If you are reading this proactively, take these steps now:

1. **Review your policy declarations page.** Know your dwelling coverage amount, your standard deductible, and whether you have a separate wind/hail deductible (increasingly common in the Philadelphia market).
2. **Document your roof's current condition.** Take clear photos of your roof from multiple angles on a clear day. Date-stamped photos of a roof in good condition are powerful evidence when filing a claim later.
3. **Keep maintenance records.** Receipts from inspections, repairs, gutter cleaning, and any roofing work demonstrate that damage is from a covered event, not neglect. Insurance adjusters look for evidence of deferred maintenance as a reason to reduce or deny claims.

## Step 1: Document the Damage Immediately

When you discover roof damage, whether from a storm, fallen tree, or sudden leak, documentation is your most important first step. This evidence forms the foundation of your entire roof insurance claim Philadelphia process.

### What to Document

- **Exterior damage:** Missing or lifted shingles, dented flashing, visible holes, broken ridge caps, damaged gutters, fallen tree limbs on or near the roof. Photograph from the ground with a zoom lens if climbing is unsafe.
- **Interior damage:** Water stains on ceilings and walls, active drips, damaged insulation visible in the attic, wet spots, mold growth. Photograph everything with good lighting.
- **Surrounding property:** Damage to siding, windows, fences, vehicles, or landscaping helps establish the severity of the storm event.
- **Timestamps:** Your phone's camera automatically timestamps photos. Additionally, note the date and time of the event and when you discovered the damage.
- **Weather data:** Save weather reports, storm warnings, and any documented wind speeds or hail size from the event. The National Weather Service archives storm data for the Philadelphia region.

### Emergency Mitigation

If there is an active leak or exposed opening, take reasonable steps to prevent further damage. Tarping a damaged area, placing buckets under leaks, and moving belongings away from water intrusion are all expected actions. Keep receipts for any emergency supplies you purchase, as these costs are typically reimbursable under your policy.

This emergency tarping and temporary repair is separate from the permanent fix and can often begin before the claim is filed.

## Step 2: Contact Your Insurance Company

File the claim as soon as possible after documenting the damage. Delays in reporting can complicate the process and, in some cases, give the insurer grounds to question the claim. When you call, here is how to file roof insurance claim notifications effectively:

### What to Say

- State that you are reporting property damage to your roof
- Provide the date and approximate time of the event
- Describe the damage in general terms (do not speculate about repair costs)
- Mention that you have photographic documentation
- Ask for a claim number and the name of the assigned adjuster
- Ask about the timeline for the adjuster inspection

### What NOT to Say

- Do not accept blame or suggest the damage may be from wear and tear
- Do not authorize any permanent repairs before the adjuster inspects
- Do not accept a settlement offer over the phone without a physical inspection
- Do not agree to use the insurance company's recommended contractor without researching alternatives

### Pennsylvania-Specific Regulations

Under Pennsylvania insurance law, your insurer must:
- Acknowledge your claim within 10 business days
- Begin investigation within 15 business days
- Make a decision within 30 days of receiving all required documentation
- Pay approved claims within 30 days of reaching a settlement

If your insurer is not meeting these timelines, you can file a complaint with the Pennsylvania Insurance Department.

## Step 3: Get an Independent Contractor Estimate

Before the insurance adjuster roof inspection, schedule an independent assessment from a licensed roofing contractor. This is one of the most important steps in the process and one that many homeowners skip, to their detriment.

### Why an Independent Estimate Matters

Insurance adjusters are employed by the insurance company. Their job is to assess damage accurately, but they are also working within a system that incentivizes keeping claim payouts manageable. An independent contractor estimate:

- Identifies damage the adjuster might miss, especially on complex Philadelphia rooflines
- Provides a real-world repair or replacement cost based on current local pricing
- Documents damage using roofing industry expertise rather than insurance industry templates
- Gives you leverage if the adjuster's estimate is unreasonably low

## Step 4: Meet the Insurance Adjuster

The insurance adjuster roof inspection is the most critical moment in the claim process. How this inspection goes often determines the outcome of your entire claim.

### Preparation for the Adjuster Visit

- **Have your contractor present if possible.** Many Philadelphia homeowners do not realize they can have their own roofing contractor on-site during the adjuster's inspection. We regularly attend adjuster inspections on behalf of our clients to ensure all damage is properly identified and documented.
- **Provide your documentation.** Share your photos, timeline, and any weather data with the adjuster.
- **Walk the property together.** Point out all areas of damage, including interior water intrusion that may not be visible from the roof.
- **Take notes.** Document what the adjuster says, where they inspect, and what damage they acknowledge.

### Common Adjuster Tactics to Watch For

We are not suggesting adjusters are adversarial, but understanding common patterns helps you advocate for yourself:

- **Partial damage assessment:** The adjuster may approve repair of one slope while ignoring matching damage on other slopes. Philadelphia roofs often sustain uniform storm damage across all exposures.
- **Repair vs. replace disputes:** An adjuster may approve patching or spot repairs when the damage pattern warrants full replacement. If shingle damage exceeds 25-30% of any slope, repair is generally impractical and replacement is the appropriate scope.
- **Depreciation deductions:** Insurance policies pay either Replacement Cost Value (RCV) or Actual Cash Value (ACV). ACV deducts depreciation based on roof age. Know which type of policy you have before the inspection.
- **Code upgrade exclusions:** Philadelphia building codes may require upgrades (ventilation, ice and water shield, drip edge) during a replacement that were not required when the original roof was installed. Some policies cover code upgrades; some do not.

## Step 5: Review the Adjuster's Report and Estimate

After the inspection, the adjuster will produce a damage report and a repair/replacement estimate, typically using Xactimate software. Review this document carefully and compare it to your independent contractor estimate.

### Common Discrepancies to Look For

- **Line items missing:** The adjuster's scope may omit items your contractor identified, such as damaged underlayment, rotted decking, or flashing replacement.
- **Below-market pricing:** Xactimate pricing does not always reflect current Philadelphia-area labor and material costs, especially after major regional storm events when demand drives prices up.
- **Incomplete removal costs:** Tear-off, disposal, dumpster rental, and dump fees should be itemized. Some adjuster estimates use unrealistically low waste removal figures.
- **Missing overhead and profit:** If you are hiring a contractor (rather than doing the work yourself), the estimate should include standard contractor overhead and profit, typically 10% and 10% respectively.

If there are significant discrepancies between the adjuster's estimate and your contractor's estimate, you have the right to negotiate. This is where having a detailed, well-documented contractor estimate from a licensed company becomes invaluable.

## Step 6: Negotiate If Necessary

Many roof insurance claim Philadelphia situations require at least some negotiation. This is normal and expected. Here is how to approach it:

### Filing a Supplement

A supplement is a formal request to revise the claim amount based on additional documentation. Common reasons for supplements include:

- Damage discovered during tear-off that was not visible during the initial inspection (rotted decking, damaged underlayment, previous improper repairs)
- Code-required upgrades identified after the original scope was set
- Material or labor cost increases between the adjuster estimate and actual repair date

Your contractor should handle supplement documentation. At Adilay Roofing, we manage the supplemental process for our clients, providing the insurance company with detailed documentation, photos, and invoicing to support the revised scope.

### Requesting Re-Inspection

If you believe the adjuster missed significant damage or made errors, you can request a re-inspection by a different adjuster. Put this request in writing and include your contractor's assessment with supporting photos.

### Hiring a Public Adjuster

For large or complex claims, hiring a public adjuster may be worthwhile. Public adjusters work for you, not the insurance company, and handle all negotiations. They typically charge 10-15% of the claim payout. This is most cost-effective for claims above $15,000-$20,000 where the insurance company's estimate is significantly lower than the actual repair cost.

## Common Roof Damage Insurance Pitfalls to Avoid

After helping hundreds of Philadelphia homeowners navigate this process, here are the mistakes we see most often:

1. **Waiting too long to file.** Most policies require reporting within a reasonable timeframe, often 60-90 days of the damage event. Filing promptly avoids disputes.
2. **Not documenting before repairs.** Emergency tarping is fine, but do not begin permanent repairs before the adjuster inspects. Taking before photos and having your contractor document conditions prior to any work protects your claim.
3. **Accepting the first offer without review.** The initial adjuster estimate is a starting point. Review it against your contractor's assessment and negotiate discrepancies.
4. **Signing over insurance proceeds to a contractor.** Legitimate contractors do not ask you to sign over your insurance check. You should control the funds and pay your contractor per your agreed contract terms.
5. **Ignoring interior damage.** Roof leaks cause water damage to ceilings, walls, insulation, and sometimes electrical systems. Make sure all related interior damage is included in the claim scope.
6. **Not understanding your deductible.** Your deductible comes out of the total approved amount. If the claim is approved for $12,000 and your deductible is $2,500, you receive $9,500. Any contractor claiming they can "cover" your deductible is engaging in insurance fraud.

## When to File a Claim vs. Pay Out of Pocket

Not every roof repair warrants an insurance claim. Filing a claim for minor damage can increase your premiums and count against you when shopping for future coverage. Consider these guidelines:

- **File a claim when** damage clearly exceeds your deductible by a significant margin, typically 2x or more. Example: $1,500 deductible with $8,000+ in damage.
- **Pay out of pocket when** damage is close to or below your deductible, or when the repair is minor and clearly affordable. Example: $2,500 deductible with $3,000 in damage.
